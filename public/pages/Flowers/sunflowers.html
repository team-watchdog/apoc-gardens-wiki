Description

The sunflower (Helianthus annuus), with its golden-yellow petals and large golden centre, is one of the cheeriest flowers to grow in the garden. There are several varieties of sunflowers.


Planting requirements

Planting season: October to early November/March to April In Sri Lanka, sunflowers can be planted during the Maha and Yala seasons.

Planting conditions:

Propagation To plant, sow the large black and white seeds about 2-3cm deep. Spacing depends on the size of the sunflower variety
Planting method Requires a well drained, moist, rich soil with lots of organic matter Sunflowers prefer loamy soils with a pH between 6.0 and 7.5.
Soil Regular moisture in the soil. Adequate moisture is important during flowering period
Water Full sunshine (at least 6 hours of direct sunlight)

Growing conditions:

Temperatures Requires a soil temperatures between 50°F and 86°F In Sri Lanka, sunflowers can tolerate temperatures between 18°C to 30°C.
Soil Add plenty of mulch Mulching helps retain moisture and suppresses weeds.
Water Water them frequently specially during dry and hot days. Water the roots and not stem, flowers or leaves Avoid wetting the foliage to prevent fungal diseases.
Pruning Propagation from the seed or cuttings

Harvesting

Can be harvested within 10-11 weeks. Once the flowers mature, petals will shed and the head becomes brownish color. Harvest individual flowers using a knife Harvest sunflowers when the backs of the flower heads turn yellow-brown.


Curing

After harvesting, allow the seeds to dry in the sun for about two weeks before storing them Curing helps reduce moisture content and prevents mold growth.


Storage

Store dried seeds in an airtight container in a cool, dark place Stored properly, sunflower seeds can last up to a year.


Protecting your plants

Pest control

Pest type: No severe pest damages known in sunflowers. Occasionally, sunflower moth can damage the immature seeds and other parts of the head.
Symptoms: Holes in leaves and flowers, damaged seeds
Control method: Spray insecticides Alternatively, use row covers to protect young plants from pests.

Disease Control

Disease type: Sclerotinia wilt (Sclerotinia sclerotiorum)
Symptoms: Wilting of leaves and stems, white mold on plant parts
Management: Practice crop rotation, remove infected plant material, avoid overhead irrigation Fungicides can be used as a last resort.


Sunflowers

Generic name: Sunflowers
Sri Lankan name: Sooriyakantha (Sinhala), Cūriyakānti (Tamil)
Scientific name: Helianthus annuus
Plant family: Asteraceae
Edible parts: Seeds, leaves, petals, roots, stalks, shells Sunflower seeds are rich in vitamins and minerals such as vitamin E, copper, zinc, selenium, phosphorus, magnesium, manganese, and thiamine. They are also high in protein and healthy fats.

Difficulty rating

Low country wet zone

Difficulty:

★★★★☆

Explanation: Sunflowers require full sunshine and well-drained soil. They can tolerate some shade but may not grow as tall or produce as many flowers.
Challenges/Adaptations:

  • Ensure adequate sunlight and drainage In the wet zone, ensure proper drainage to prevent waterlogging.

Low country dry zone

Difficulty:

★★★☆☆

Explanation: Sunflowers are drought-tolerant once established, making them suitable for the dry zone. However, they require consistent moisture during flowering.
Challenges/Adaptations:

  • Provide regular watering during flowering period In the dry zone, provide additional irrigation during flowering to ensure proper pollination.

Mid country

Difficulty:

★★★★☆

Explanation: The mid-country climate is suitable for sunflowers, but they may require some protection from strong winds.
Challenges/Adaptations:

  • Provide windbreaks if necessary Windbreaks can help prevent flower heads from being damaged by strong winds.

Up country

Difficulty:

★★★★☆

Explanation: Sunflowers can grow in the upcountry, but they may not tolerate frost and require careful monitoring of temperatures.
Challenges/Adaptations:

  • Protect from frost; monitor temperatures In the upcountry, provide protection during cold nights to prevent frost damage.

Companion plants

Companion plants aid growth by attracting beneficial insects, repelling pests, and providing support. They are part of a natural pest control system, maximising space and increasing crop productivity.

  • Cucumbers
  • Melons
  • Sweetcorn
  • Squash

Non-companion plants

Non-companion plants are those that negatively affect each other’s growth when planted together, often due to competition for resources or chemical interference. Avoiding these pairings helps improve plant health and yields.

  • Potatoes Sunflowers can inhibit the growth of potatoes due to their allelopathic properties.
