Welcome

This is an online wiki of growable plants in Sri Lanka. After our investigations into agriculture in 2022, we realized two things:

  1. Sri Lanka can actually grow a huge variety of food crops, despite our general focus being on a handful of monocrops. The conditions are excellent, especially for small-scale gardening - from balconies to backyards.
  2. Our agriculture supply chains are extremely fragile, powered largely by large-scale fertilizer imports, and could use buttressing - the more we can grow locally, the more stress we can take off both ourselves, the economy, and the ecosystem.
  3. Knowledge about growing varied food crops is surprisingly difficult to find here. Almost every home garden has lots of ornamental plants and some chilies, but it was very hard to find simple, easy-to-read instructions of how to get some edibles out of the ground.

This is an effort to address this problem. Each entry shows you data about the plant in question, how to plant it, how to make it grow, how to use it - and also how it supports other plants in the ecosystem. It's meant for beginner gardeners, so we've sacrificed extreme detail and yields for practical advice sourced from all over the globe and filtered with our specific conditions in mind.

A few things to note:

  1. We use a five-point subjective rating scale for difficulty.

    This scale is compiled by Watchdog staff by examining the planting, growing, and harvesting requirements of each plant, and comparing them against the general conditions of various climatic zones in Sri Lanka.

    You can interpret it as follows:

    • 1 star: this will grow whether you want it to or not
    • 2 stars: if you can get this plant germinating, it'll be fine
    • 3 stars: you'll need to put in some effort and regular care, and maybe some fertilizer
    • 4 stars: you're going to need to modify the plant's environment to get it growing properly
    • 5 stars: expect to carefully regulate temperature, water intake, do lots of monitoring, and be on hand with a lot of fertilizer

  2. You'll note that we talk about organic pesticides.

    We also talk about companion plants: the idea that growing is more sustainable when plant species support each other. These are ideas taken from permaculture.

    A common example are the "Three Sisters" of Indigenous American agriculture - corn, beans and squash, planted together. The corn is the main food crop and provides a support for the beans to grow; the beans fix nitrogen into the soil, providing nutrients to the corn and squash; the squash keeps pests away. You'll find companion and non-companion plants listed for each entry. You don't need these when growing, but they're useful.

  3. You will need to do some soil prep and some pre-work before you plant.

    Much of a crop's success depends on the soil it grows in. We've written an entire First Principles article here that you can refer to for building soil, picking an initial set of plants, creating your own compost, and even cheap irrigation setups.

  4. We encourage you to read and study further as you go along. To that effect, here are the Sources we use!

Enjoy!
